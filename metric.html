01Interview⌁

1. Tell me about yourself

I am a Senior Data and AI Engineer with more than eight years of experience building enterprise data platforms, analytics solutions and AI-powered applications. At RTX, I lead data and AI initiatives involving Snowflake governance, executive reporting, data-quality standardisation and MCP-based AI solutions. At Walmart, I built monitoring and automation platforms supporting more than 26,000 data pipelines, improving SLA performance from approximately 95% to 98%. My strength is connecting data engineering, governance, business metrics and AI so that leaders and end users can make decisions using trusted data. This Smartsheet role is a strong match because it combines semantic layers, executive reporting, metric governance, self-service analytics and AI agents.

02Foundations⌁

2. What this role is about

This role is about creating a trusted enterprise data layer that supports executive scorecards, self-service dashboards and AI agents. The key objective is to define business metrics once, govern them centrally and make them reusable across Tableau, Amplitude, Snowflake, Databricks and AI tools.

The three pillars are:

1. Executive reporting
2. Metric governance and standardisation
3. Self-service and AI-powered analytics

Data-quality tooling

Quality tooling answers:

Can this data currently be trusted?

Important checks:

Completeness — required values are present
Uniqueness — business keys are unique
Validity — values follow accepted rules
Consistency — systems agree
Accuracy — values reconcile with authoritative sources
Freshness — data arrives on time
Referential integrity — relationships are valid

Strong statement:

The catalogue explains the data, while quality tooling validates the data. The catalogue should display the latest quality and freshness status.

07KPI⌁

7. Numerator and denominator

Metric = Numerator ÷ Denominator

The numerator is the outcome being measured.

The denominator is the eligible population or comparison base.

Example:

Activation rate =
Activated new accounts
──────────────────────
Eligible new accounts

Always clarify:

Who is eligible?
What time period applies?
Which exclusions apply?
Is the denominator fixed or changing?

For count metrics such as active customers, there may be no denominator.

20Interview⌁

20. Handling conflicting metric definitions

Question:

Finance, Product and Customer Success define active customers differently. What would you do?

Answer:

I would first understand the business question each team is answering. If the concepts are genuinely different, I would not force one metric. I would create clearly named metrics such as active paid customer, monthly active product account and customer-success-managed account. Each would have a precise definition, owner, grain, exclusions and authoritative source. If the teams are trying to answer the same question, I would facilitate agreement, reconcile the data and certify one enterprise definition.

21. Designing a new KPI

Use this sequence:

1. Identify the decision it supports
2. Define the business outcome
3. Specify numerator and denominator
4. Define grain and time window
5. Set inclusion and exclusion rules
6. Identify authoritative sources
7. Select approved dimensions
8. Build the governed model
9. Add quality tests
10. Reconcile and validate
11. Assign ownership
12. Certify and publish
13. Monitor usage and quality
27Interview⌁

27. High-value interview phrases

Define once and consume everywhere.

Business teams own meaning; data teams own reliable implementation.

The semantic layer owns metric logic, while the AI agent interprets intent.

Governance should create trust without becoming a delivery bottleneck.

Self-service means controlled and reusable access, not unrestricted raw-data access.

A KPI should support a decision, not merely produce a number.

A metric is not certified until its definition, ownership, quality and reconciliation are complete.

Before investigating a business anomaly, I first confirm that it is not a data-quality or instrumentation issue.

Governance does not always mean forcing one definition. Sometimes it means clearly distinguishing metrics that answer different questions.

Executive reporting should explain what changed, why it changed and what action is required.
